## What Is a Phishing Email?

A phishing email is a fake message designed to trick you into giving away your password, personal information, or money. Phishing emails look like they come from real companies or people you know.

**The goal:** Get you to click a link, open an attachment, or reply with sensitive information.

---

## Quick Checks: 5 Red Flags

If you see ANY of these, the email is likely a phishing attempt:

| Red Flag | What to Look For |
|----------|------------------|
| 🚩 **Urgency** | "Act now!" "Your account will be suspended!" "24 hours to respond!" |
| 🚩 **Wrong sender** | The email address does not match who they claim to be |
| 🚩 **Suspicious links** | Hover over links — the destination does not match the text |
| 🚩 **Unexpected attachments** | You were not expecting a file, especially .zip, .exe, or .docm |
| 🚩 **Requests for personal info** | "Verify your password" "Confirm your credit card" |

## Step-by-Step: How to Check an Email

When you receive an email that seems suspicious, do these checks:

### Step 1: Check the Sender's Email Address

Do not trust the display name. Check the actual email address.

1. Look at the sender's email address carefully
2. Watch for slight misspellings:
   - ❌ microsft.com (missing 'o')
   - ❌ amaz0n.com (zero instead of 'o')
   - ❌ paypa1.com (number 1 instead of 'l')
   - ✅ microsoft.com
   - ✅ amazon.com
   - ✅ paypal.com
3. Legitimate companies do not use @gmail.com, @yahoo.com, or @outlook.com for official emails

**Example:**
- Display name says: "Microsoft Support"
- But email address is: microsoft-support@gmail.com
- This is a phishing email. Microsoft never uses Gmail.

### Step 2: Hover Over Links (Do NOT Click)

Always check where a link really goes before clicking.

1. Move your mouse cursor over the link or button
2. **Do not click** — just hover
3. Look at the bottom-left corner of your screen
4. The real destination URL will appear there

**Example:**
- The email says: "Click here to login: www.yourbank.com"
- But hovering shows: www.yourbank.fake-site.com/login
- This is a phishing link. The real domain is "fake-site.com", not "yourbank.com".

### Step 3: Look for Urgency and Threats

Phishing emails try to make you panic so you act without thinking.

**Common phishing phrases:**
- "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours"
- "Unauthorized login detected — verify now"
- "You have won a prize! Claim within 1 hour"
- "Your password has expired — update immediately"
- "Urgent: payment required to avoid legal action"

**The rule:** Real companies give you time. They do not threaten you over email.

### Step 4: Check for Poor Writing

Many phishing emails have obvious errors:

- Spelling mistakes
- Bad grammar
- Strange formatting
- Generic greetings like "Dear Customer" instead of your name
- Inconsistent fonts or logos that look slightly wrong

**The rule:** Professional companies proofread their emails. Errors are a red flag.

### Step 5: Ask Yourself These Questions

- Was I expecting this email?
- Do I know the sender?
- Does the email ask for my password, personal info, or money?
- Would this company really contact me this way?

If the answer to any question makes you suspicious, **do not click anything**.

## What to Do If You Suspect Phishing

| Action | How To Do It |
|--------|-------------|
| **Do not click** | Do not click links, open attachments, or reply |
| **Report it** | Use the "Report Phishing" button in Outlook, or forward to IT |
| **Delete it** | After reporting, delete the email |

**In Outlook:**
1. Select the suspicious email
2. Click **"Report Message"** in the top toolbar
3. Select **"Phishing"**
4. The email is sent to your security team and removed from your inbox

## What If You Already Clicked?

Do not panic. Act quickly:

1. **If you only clicked a link** (did not enter any info):
   - Close the browser tab immediately
   - Run a virus scan (Windows Security > Virus & threat protection > Quick scan)
   - Report the email as phishing

2. **If you entered your password on a fake site:**
   - Change your password immediately from a different device
   - Go to the real website by typing the URL yourself (not from the email)
   - Report it to IT security immediately
   - Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) if not already on

3. **If you entered financial information:**
   - Contact your bank immediately
   - Report the fraud
   - Monitor your accounts for suspicious activity

## Real Examples of Phishing Emails

**Example 1: The Fake IT Support**
> Subject: Password Expiring Today
> Body: "Your password expires in 2 hours. Click here to keep your current password."
> Red flags: Urgency, request to click a link to "keep" password, poor grammar.

**Example 2: The Fake Invoice**
> Subject: Invoice Attached — Payment Required
> Body: "Please find your invoice attached. Payment is overdue."
> Red flags: Unexpected attachment, you were not expecting an invoice, generic greeting.

**Example 3: The Fake Boss**
> Subject: Urgent Request
> Body: "I'm in a meeting and need you to buy gift cards. Reply with the codes."
> Red flags: Unusual request from a manager, urgency, asks for gift cards.

## Prevention Tips

- Always check the sender's email address — not just the display name
- Hover over links before clicking — check the real destination
- If unsure, contact the sender through a different method (phone, chat, new email)
- Never give your password to anyone — IT will never ask for it via email
- Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) on your accounts
- When in doubt, report it — it is better to report a legitimate email than to miss a phishing one

## When to Call the Helpdesk

Contact IT support immediately if:

- You clicked a link or opened an attachment in a suspicious email
- You entered your password or personal information on a suspicious website
- You are unsure whether an email is legitimate
- You received a phishing email that looks like it came from inside your organization

## FAQ

**Q: What if the email looks completely real?**
**A:** Sophisticated phishing emails can look very convincing. The sender address and link destinations are the hardest things to fake. Always check those first, even if everything else looks perfect.

**Q: Can phishing emails come from people I know?**
**A:** Yes. If a colleague's account is compromised, attackers can send phishing emails from their real address. If the message seems unusual for that person, verify through another channel (call them, send a new email, message them on Teams).

**Q: Is it safe to click "Unsubscribe" on a phishing email?**
**A:** No. Clicking "unsubscribe" confirms to the attacker that your email address is active. This can lead to more phishing attempts. Instead, report the email as phishing and delete it.
