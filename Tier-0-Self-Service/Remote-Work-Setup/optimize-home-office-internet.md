## Tip 1: Place Your Router in the Best Location

Where you put your router makes a huge difference to signal strength.

**The best place for your router:**
- **Central location:** In the middle of your home, not in a corner
- **Elevated:** On a shelf or table, not on the floor
- **Open space:** Not inside a cabinet, behind a TV, or blocked by furniture
- **Near your workspace:** The closer to where you work, the better

**Places to avoid:**
- Behind or under a desk
- Inside a cupboard or cabinet
- Near large metal objects (filing cabinet, refrigerator)
- On the floor
- In the basement if your office is upstairs

**Quick test:** Move closer to your router and check if your internet improves. If it does, distance or obstacles are the problem.

**What to tell yourself:** "Wi-Fi is like a light bulb — the farther you are, the dimmer it gets. Walls, floors, and furniture all block the signal. Even moving the router a few feet can make a big difference."

## Tip 2: Reduce Wi-Fi Interference

Common household items can interfere with your Wi-Fi signal.

**Things that block or weaken Wi-Fi:**
- Microwave ovens (when running)
- Cordless phones (older models especially)
- Baby monitors
- Bluetooth speakers and devices
- Large mirrors and fish tanks
- Thick concrete or brick walls
- Metal filing cabinets and shelves

**What you can do:**
1. Move your router away from these items
2. Move your workspace away from these items
3. Keep the path between your router and computer as clear as possible
4. Do not place the router next to other electronic devices

**What to tell yourself:** "Wi-Fi competes with other wireless signals. If my router is next to a microwave, my internet drops every time someone heats up lunch."

## Tip 3: Choose the Right Wi-Fi Band (2.4 GHz vs 5 GHz)

Most modern routers have two Wi-Fi networks. Choosing the right one matters.

| Feature | 2.4 GHz | 5 GHz |
|---------|:-------:|:-----:|
| Range | Longer (better through walls) | Shorter (weaker through walls) |
| Speed | Slower | Faster |
| Interference | More (crowded) | Less (less crowded) |
| Best for | Far from router | Close to router |

**How to choose:**
1. Look at your Wi-Fi network list — you may see two network names:
   - "MyNetwork" (2.4 GHz)
   - "MyNetwork-5G" (5 GHz)
2. If you are **close to the router** (same room): use **5 GHz** for faster speed
3. If you are **far from the router** (different floor, multiple walls): use **2.4 GHz** for better range
4. Try both and see which gives you better video call quality

**What to tell yourself:** "5 GHz is faster but doesn't travel far. 2.4 GHz is slower but reaches further. I'll try both and pick the one that works best in my workspace."

## Tip 4: Limit Other Internet Use During Important Calls

Your internet is shared by every device in your home. During video calls, ask others to limit heavy use.

**Activities that use lots of internet:**
- Streaming video (Netflix, YouTube, Disney+)
- Online gaming
- Large file downloads
- Cloud backups and sync
- Video calls on other devices

**What to do before an important call:**
1. Ask family members to pause streaming and downloads
2. Pause your own cloud sync (OneDrive, Google Drive, Dropbox)
3. Close browser tabs playing video or audio
4. Turn off your personal phone's Wi-Fi if it is syncing photos or updates
5. Use your phone's cellular data for personal browsing during the call

**What to tell yourself:** "My internet is like a water pipe — if someone else is taking a shower, my water pressure drops. During important calls, I'll ask others to limit their internet use."

## Tip 5: Test Your Internet Speed

Knowing your speed helps you understand what you can and cannot do.

**How to test:**
1. Go to **speedtest.net** or **fast.com**
2. Click the "Go" or "Start" button
3. Wait for the test to complete (about 30 seconds)
4. Note your **download** and **upload** speeds

**What the numbers mean for video calls:**

| Speed | What You Can Do |
|-------|----------------|
| Download under 3 Mbps | Basic browsing, email |
| Download 3-10 Mbps | Video calls (1-on-1), streaming |
| Download 10+ Mbps | Group video calls, HD video |
| Upload under 1 Mbps | May struggle with video calls |
| Upload 1-3 Mbps | Standard video calls |
| Upload 3+ Mbps | HD video calls, screen sharing |

**If your speeds are low:**
- Contact your Internet Service Provider about a faster plan
- Ask specifically about **upload speed** — many plans have fast download but slow upload
- Video calls need good upload speed to send your video to others

**What to tell yourself:** "If my upload speed is under 1 Mbps, video calls will always struggle. I may need to upgrade my internet plan."

## Tip 6: Use a Wired Connection When Possible

A wired connection is always more reliable than Wi-Fi.

**What you need:**
- An **Ethernet cable** (looks like a thick phone cord)
- An Ethernet port on your computer (most laptops and all desktops have one)
- If your laptop doesn't have an Ethernet port, you can buy a USB-to-Ethernet adapter

**How to connect:**
1. Plug one end of the Ethernet cable into your computer
2. Plug the other end into your router (look for numbered ports on the back)
3. Windows should switch automatically to the wired connection
4. Turn off Wi-Fi to ensure the wired connection is used: click the Wi-Fi icon > turn off Wi-Fi

**Benefits of wired:**
- No Wi-Fi interference
- No signal drops
- Faster and more stable
- Best for important meetings and presentations

**What to tell yourself:** "Wired is always better than Wi-Fi. For important meetings, I'll plug in directly if I can. Even a long Ethernet cable is more reliable than the best Wi-Fi."

## Quick Pre-Meeting Checklist

Before an important video call:

- [ ] Restart your router (unplug, wait 60 seconds, plug back in)
- [ ] Move closer to the router if using Wi-Fi
- [ ] Ask others to pause streaming and downloads
- [ ] Close unused browser tabs and applications
- [ ] Run a speed test to confirm your connection is good
- [ ] Use a wired connection if available

## When to Call the Helpdesk

Contact IT support if:

- Your internet speed is consistently below requirements for video calls
- You have tried all tips and video calls still freeze or drop
- You need help setting up a wired connection
- Your router is more than 5 years old and needs replacement
- You need a USB-to-Ethernet adapter ordered

## FAQ

**Q: How fast does my internet need to be for video calls?**
**A:** For standard video calls, you need at least 1-2 Mbps upload and download. For group calls with HD video, aim for 3-5 Mbps upload and download. Test your speed at speedtest.net.

**Q: Will upgrading my internet plan fix my video call problems?**
**A:** Only if your current speed is too slow. If your speed is already adequate (5+ Mbps), the problem is more likely Wi-Fi signal or router placement. Upgrade your plan if your upload speed is below 2 Mbps.

**Q: My internet is fast but video calls still freeze. Why?**
**A:** Video calls need consistent, steady internet — not just fast speed. Wi-Fi interference, other devices using bandwidth, or router problems can cause freezing even on fast connections. Try a wired connection.
