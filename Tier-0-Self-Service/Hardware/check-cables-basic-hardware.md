## Before You Call the Helpdesk

Many hardware problems are caused by loose or disconnected cables. Checking your connections solves most issues in minutes.

**IMPORTANT:** Before touching any cables, look at how they are connected. If you are unsure, take a photo with your phone first so you can reconnect them the same way.

## Fix 1: Computer Won't Turn On

**Step 1: Check the power cable**
1. Look at the back of your computer — is the power cable firmly plugged in?
2. Follow the cable to the wall outlet — is it plugged in securely?
3. Check both ends of the cable — sometimes the end at the computer comes loose

**Step 2: Check the power source**
1. Is the power strip or surge protector turned ON? Look for a small switch or light
2. Test the wall outlet by plugging in something you know works — like a lamp or phone charger
3. If using a laptop, is the charger connected? Look for the charging light on the laptop

**Step 3: Try a different outlet**
1. Plug the computer directly into a different wall outlet
2. Bypass the power strip completely — plug directly into the wall

**Step 4: Perform a hard reset**
1. Unplug the power cable from the computer
2. Press and hold the power button for 30 seconds
3. Release the power button
4. Plug the power cable back in
5. Press the power button normally

**What to tell yourself:** "Power problems are the most common reason a computer won't start. I'll check every connection from the wall to the computer before calling IT."

## Fix 2: Monitor Shows Black Screen

**Step 1: Check the monitor is on**
1. Look for a small light on the monitor — usually on the bottom edge
2. If there is no light at all, press the power button on the monitor
3. If the light is amber or orange, the monitor is on but not receiving a signal from the computer

**Step 2: Check the video cable**
1. Look at the back of the monitor and the back of the computer
2. Is the cable firmly connected at both ends?
3. Common cable types: HDMI (flat, wide), DisplayPort (rectangular with one corner cut), VGA (blue, with screws)
4. Tighten any screws on the cable connectors

**Step 3: Check the input source**
1. Use the buttons on your monitor to open the on-screen menu
2. Find "Input Source" or "Source Select"
3. Make sure the selected input matches your cable type (HDMI 1, HDMI 2, DisplayPort, etc.)

**Step 4: For laptops with external monitors**
1. Press **Windows key + P** on your keyboard
2. A menu appears on the right side of the screen
3. Select **"Duplicate"** or **"Extend"**
4. Do not select "PC screen only"

**What to tell yourself:** "The monitor might be on but set to the wrong input — like a TV that's on the wrong channel. I'll check the input source in the monitor's menu."

## Fix 3: Keyboard Not Working

**Step 1: Check the connection**
- **Wired keyboard:** Is the USB plug fully inserted? Try a different USB port
- **Wireless keyboard:** Check the batteries. Replace them if they are old
- **Bluetooth keyboard:** Go to Settings > Bluetooth & devices > check the keyboard is connected

**Step 2: Turn the keyboard off and on**
- Wireless keyboards have a power switch — turn it off, wait 5 seconds, turn it back on
- For USB keyboards, unplug and plug back in

**Step 3: Restart your computer**
- If the keyboard stops responding completely, restart the computer
- Use the mouse to click Start > Power > Restart

**Step 4: Check for stuck keys**
- Look at each key — is anything stuck underneath?
- Gently clean around keys with compressed air or a soft brush
- Turn the keyboard upside down and gently shake out any debris

**What to tell yourself:** "If it's wireless, it's probably the batteries. If it's wired, it's probably a loose USB connection."

## Fix 4: Mouse Not Working

**Step 1: Check the connection**
- **Wired mouse:** Is the USB plug fully inserted? Try a different USB port
- **Wireless mouse:** Check the batteries. The most common cause is dead batteries
- **Bluetooth mouse:** Go to Settings > Bluetooth & devices > check the mouse is connected

**Step 2: Check the surface**
- Optical mice need a non-reflective surface
- Try using the mouse on a different surface — a piece of paper or a mousepad
- Glass and very shiny surfaces can prevent the mouse from tracking

**Step 3: Clean the mouse**
- Turn the mouse over and look at the sensor (the small light on the bottom)
- If there is dust or hair on the sensor, gently wipe it clean
- Clean the mouse feet (small pads on the bottom) with a damp cloth

**Step 4: Restart your computer**
- If the mouse is completely unresponsive, restart the computer
- Use the keyboard to navigate: press Windows key, use arrow keys to reach Power > Restart

**What to tell yourself:** "If the cursor is jerky or not moving, check the surface first — optical mice need a non-reflective surface to work properly."

## Fix 5: USB Device Not Recognized

This applies to USB drives, external hard drives, webcams, headsets, and printers.

**Step 1: Try a different USB port**
1. Unplug the device
2. Plug it into a different USB port on your computer
3. Wait 10-15 seconds for your computer to recognize it
4. Listen for the connection sound (a short chime)

**Step 2: Avoid USB hubs**
1. If the device is plugged into a USB hub, unplug it
2. Plug it directly into a USB port on the computer itself
3. USB hubs can have power issues that prevent devices from working

**Step 3: Check for the device in File Explorer**
1. Open File Explorer (folder icon)
2. Look under "This PC" for your device
3. USB drives and external hard drives appear here
4. Printers, webcams, and headsets appear in their respective settings

**Step 4: Restart your computer**
- If the device still is not recognized, restart with the device plugged in

**What to tell yourself:** "USB ports can stop working. Trying a different port — especially one on the back of the computer — often fixes the problem."

## Fix 6: No Internet (Wired Connection)

**Step 1: Check the Ethernet cable**
1. Look at the back of your computer — is the network cable plugged in?
2. The cable looks like a thick phone cord with a clear plastic connector
3. Follow the cable to your router or wall network port
4. Check both ends are firmly connected

**Step 2: Check the lights on the cable**
1. Look at where the cable plugs into your computer
2. You should see small lights — one solid, one blinking
3. No lights means no connection — the cable may be faulty

**Step 3: Restart your router**
1. Unplug your router's power cable
2. Wait 60 seconds
3. Plug it back in
4. Wait 2-3 minutes for all lights to stabilize
5. Check if internet is working

**Step 4: Try Wi-Fi as a backup**
1. Click the network icon in the system tray (bottom-right corner)
2. Look for available Wi-Fi networks
3. Connect to your Wi-Fi network if available

**What to tell yourself:** "If the Ethernet cable has no lights, there's no connection. I'll check both ends of the cable and restart the router."

## When to Call the Helpdesk

Contact IT support if:

- You checked all cables and the device still does not work
- There is visible damage to cables, ports, or the device itself
- You hear unusual sounds (clicking, grinding, buzzing) from your computer
- You smell burning or see smoke — turn off the device immediately
- Multiple devices stopped working at the same time
- The device is under warranty — do not open the case

---

## Prevention Tips

- Label your cables so you know which is which
- Take a photo of your cable setup before disconnecting anything
- Keep cables organized and away from foot traffic
- Check cable connections monthly — they can loosen over time
- Replace frayed or damaged cables immediately
